Success Stories ~ Marisol Duarte-Clark

After a few minutes talking to Marisol Duarte-Clark, you start to wonder, when does she sleep?

In addition taking 18 units, Marisol, a multiple subject credential student, splits her time between student teaching second graders at Jesse Sanchez Elementary School in Salinas and working in an after-school program at Alianza Charter School in Watsonville. Oh, and on top of that, she's producing a play.

Born and raised in San Jose, Marisol attended a junior college in Cupertino and then transferred to UC Santa Cruz to complete her undergraduate degree in theatre arts with an emphasis in modern dance. After two years working as a substitute teacher, she decided to earn her teaching credential. She was drawn to CSUMB because of the Vision Statement and the mission of the School of Education.

"The education department is really geared toward teaching populations that are in need—students who have been disenfranchised, who are low income, or live in urban settings. We learn how to really teach and work with these populations of students given structures like No Child Left Behind," says Marisol.

For the last two years, Marisol has put those skills to work by teaching drama and dance in after school programs in Watsonville geared toward the children of migrant farmworkers. "Alianza is one of the only bilingual dual immersion programs—half the curriculum is in Spanish and half is in English."

As a student teacher, Marisol works with a similar student population in Salinas. "Jesse E. Sanchez is also a predominantly migrant school, and they have even less resources than the school in Watsonville."

Carolina Serna, one of Marisol's professors, commented, "When collaborating with classmates [Marisol] made insightful contributions, often sharing experiences from her own teaching and other community involvement."

"I specifically go to these places," Marisol says, "because that is my whole mission—to learn how to work with children who are at risk, children who come from unprivileged backgrounds, who have been disenfranchised."

Marisol can relate to many of the challenges facing her students. "I didn't grow up as a migrant worker, but I did grow up in the barrio and I did grow up on free lunch with a single mom who raised three kids. I'm hoping to be some sort of inspiration to my family as well as the community. It's where my motivation comes from…it hasn't' been easy for me at all, but I think that because of that, I know exactly what these students need—not that I was migrant, but I can completely understand what it is like to live off free lunch and struggle."

Marisol's background in theater is an important element of this mission: "You really can do miracles with dance and theater without any money, I mean, it'd be nice to have sets and costumes, but theater is one of those things that can empower children to deal with their issues and allow for creative expression without costing a lot of money. And that is something you got to keep in mind when you are working with children who don't always have food for dinner or a place to live."

In addition to empowering children, Marisol is using theater and dance to build community on campus. She's in the student club Ollin, the bailefolklorico dance group on campus, and incorporating this style of dance into a play she's producing called The Panza Monologues. The play primarily deals with female body image—panza refers to the belly, womb, and the rolls of fat so often found there.

"I'm organizing this because I wanted to see Chicana theater happen on this campus, and create a space for female multiculturalism.

"There are plenty of other Chicana plays, but my favorite, Real Women Have Curves, only has six characters. I wanted to have one that could incorporate as many women as possible, like the Vagina Monologues, so that it can be a unifying event.

"I was trying to create this network of women of color. It's a very bonding, community building experience."

Marisol secured funding for the play from the Interclub Council, but she is still struggling to pull together all the necessary resources for the production. "You have to work with dancers, actors, set designers, lighting people, the PR person, the director, and publicity…it's endless.

"Although I might get grey hairs and I've been met with tons of challenges and obstacles, I am so determined. I've wanted to throw my hat in a few times, but I can't. There's no way I'm going to back out."

Shannon Edwards, a Teledramatic Arts and Technology professor, has gotten her class involved in the play. She explains what its like to working with Marisol: "As the Producer of Panza Monologues, Marisol can make anything happen! As a graduate of UCSC where community based theatre is thriving, she used her ingenuity and "can do" attitude to create new collaborations at CSUMB. She has a true gift for discovering the community treasures here at CSUMB (ie. amazingly talented students from all parts of the campus). Her energy is boundless and contagious. I hope all of the campus gets to see this important play this weekend at the BBC."

The Panza Monologues runs in the BBC on April 27 and 28, 2007 at 7:30 p.m. Marisol plans to spend her summer teaching and studying for the RICA, and after that she only has one more class. "People might think I'm crazy. Normally people would never do this, but I am grateful the school is letting me pursue what it is that I need right now, which is to incorporate the arts into my pedagogy.

"I'm following my own path and doing what it is I need to do to become a better teacher. I've gained some amazing tools on how to teach in this program."

Perhaps after that, Marisol will finally be able to get some sleep.

Update on Marisol: After taking a year off to work and save money, Marisol returned to CSUMB in Fall 2008 to study full-time and complete her credential. When she finishes up at CSUMB, she will join the American Musical Theater of San Jose as a playwrighting instructor for youth. On top of that, she's writing a proposal to teach a workshop on Chicano theater at the 2009 American Alliance for Theater Education conference in New York City as well as applying to help a Detroit youth theater group develop a play with Chicano youth.

~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer/Web Editor
