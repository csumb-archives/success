Dan Lyons '04 

Following his graduation from CSUMB, Daniel Lyons ('04 SBS) entered the Peace Corps and has been living and working in Mozambique ever since.

A beautiful country located in Southeast Africa alongside the Indian Ocean, Mozambique gained its independence from Portuguese colonial rule in 1975. Following independence the nation experienced a civil war lasting until 1992 which displaced millions of people from their homes. Making matters worse, in 2000, a cyclone hit the country and caused the largest flood ever recorded in Africa, leaving half a million people homeless.

Daniel's Peace Corps experience began with nine weeks of intensive training in the village of Massaca. There, he took intensive lessons in Portuguese and was trained on community health issues, including working with adolescents, orphans and vulnerable children, and people living with HIV/AIDS and their families.

After completing training, Daniel traveled to the island of Incaha and began working with Habitat for Humanity's International Orphans and Vulnerable Children Project.

The United Nations estimates that 17% of the population of Mozambique is infected with HIV/AIDS and the majority of those are women. The deaths resulting from AIDS have lead to an orphan crisis and large numbers of vulnerable children who are left without adequate shelter or, more often, homeless. In Incaha, Daniel designed and implemented a project to build emergency housing for these children.

During this process Daniel learned that the homes, built out of cement blocks, cost too much for many members of the community and that many existing homeowners struggled to make their mortgage payments. With the help of another Peace Corps volunteer, Daniel developed a new building method that utilizes a traditional building material, the bamboo-like canico reed, reinforced with a concrete foundation and zinc roof. This brought the cost of a house down by 50 percent. Money from a donation-based intervention fund further alleviated the crisis: Habitat was able to build homes using the new design and only require residents to pay back 30 percent of the homes' cost—and residents were able to repay that amount by working on construction of new houses.

As part of a related initiative, Daniel taught nutritional gardening techniques to the women who lead households of orphaned and vulnerable children. By growing and consuming vegetables like beets and carrots that support the immune system, the families can lead healthier lives. As an incentive, women who maintained gardens and trained others in gardening techniques received mortgage discounts.

In addition, Daniel worked with the local government, other aid agencies, and community-based organizations in the area to build a stronger welfare infrastructure in the region. As a result of these partnerships, the village built a new HIV testing center and developed a home-based care program.

In an effort to address the social aspects of the AIDS pandemic in Mozambique, Daniel worked with two other Peace Corps volunteers to organize an annual conference for young men from across the nation called JOMA (Jovens Para Mudanca e Accao/Youth for Social Movement and Action). During the conference young men examine the cultural norms of Mozambique and identify unhealthy gender roles and stereotypes, with the goal of encouraging positive behavior changes. The men and their teachers then return to their home communities and implement a project to share what they have learned and promote healthier behavior among their peers.

Though the issues facing Mozambique will require work for years to come, Dan has made an important and positive impact already.


~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer/Web Editor
