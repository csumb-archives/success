Cities' rich cultures bloom in murals

Johanna Poethig, a professor of painting and public art, was on professional leave during the Fall 2005 semester, and it's clear she put the time to good use.

She finished two large mural projects, one in Chicago and the other in Vancouver, Washington.

Poethig is an internationally exhibited artist, who, according to Jon Pounds of the Chicago Public Art Group, "would make a short list of the most significant muralists in the country."

She designed and led the painting of "Loop Tattoo," a 6,500-square-foot community mural that covers the side of a building on East Lake Street just off Michigan Avenue. She and a dozen artists from the Chicago Public Art Group painted the mural, which measures about 50 feet wide and 135 feet long, on 270 5-by-5-foot square mats, which were mounted to the wall.

According to The Chicago Tribune, the mural (viewable online) begins about 240 feet off the ground. Near the top, a pink sky framed by leaves and flowers gives way to a central swirl of figures-dancers, jazz musicians, and athletes-against a vivid green background. In the lower third of the mural, the border of leaves and branches turns into interlacing rings or loops.

The figures and foliage are meant to represent the rich cultural life and greening of the Loop, Poethig, 49, told The Tribune.

"I'm a site-specific artist. I look for artifacts or symbols of the city," she said.

Poethig's beliefs sync with the social mission of public art-to claim public spaces and transform them-principles she teaches at the Visual and Public Art Department at CSUMB.

"Taking a big public work outside takes space from advertising, and we're able to see messages and pictures that aren't trying to sell us something," she said. "Putting something in a public space that's art is a way for us to reclaim our own space."

In Vancouver, she worked on a two-story mural that graces the lobby of a new building on the campus of Washington State University-a mural that pays homage to local geography, history, and education called "Flight of the Mind, Exploration of the Heart."

According to her proposal for the project, she hoped to "represent the study of science and technology while referring to mysterious patterns of life."

The Columbia River is depicted across the bottom of the mural; on one shore, a large bear can be seen clutching a salmon.

A gifted painter, Poethig thrives on community-created art, which allows her to draw from other people's experiences and translate them visually, she said.

According to Poethig, community art battles the myth of artist as isolated male genius. "I believe in breaking that down and including people of all cultures and backgrounds."

~ Joan Weiner, News and Public Information Officer
