Julio Cardona '03

Here at CSUMB, many students are among the first-generation of their family to attend college.

This was the case for Lucia Cardona-Raya, who decided to earn her college degree at age 40, becoming one of the original pioneer Otters. She earned her B.A. in Liberal Studies in 1997, and then continued on at CSUMB, earning her teaching credential and pursuing a master's. Sadly, Lucia passed away due to cancer in 2001, shortly before graduation. She received her master's degree in education posthumously.

Lucia's oldest son, Julio, was inspired by his mother's commitment to education. Though he already had a culinary arts degree and had taken some courses at MPC, Julio decided to enroll at CSUMB as a way to both honor Lucia and become a better role model for his younger brother. Two years later he graduated with his degree in Human Communication, becoming the very first second-generation Otter.

Like his mother, Julio realized the enormous power of education, and decided to devote his life to it. As a CSUMB student, he became fascinated with the experiences of first-generation college students and worked closely with Professors Rina Benmayor and Cecilia O'Leary on the First Generation College Student Archive, a collection of the oral histories of first generation CSUMB students. This experience as an undergrad ultimately shaped the trajectory of Julio's career.

Following his graduation from CSUMB, Julio went on to Stanford to earn his master's degree in education. "What got me going was working with the first generation research going on at CSUMB," says Julio. "I wanted to continue doing more research-based work on access issues in higher education and looking at different retention issues in college." For his master's thesis, he analyzed academic support systems for first generation college students. Today, he utilizes that expertise as the assistant dean of graduate studies at UC Santa Cruz.

"I love working with students, especially first-generation students. To start with high school students and inspire them to go to college, and then to continue on, so that they don't just go to college and drop out, or go and get their bachelors degree, but continue on, to hopefully get a master's degree and a Ph.D."

At UCSC, Julio oversees several programs that support graduate students, particularly underrepresented students-first generation students, students of color, and women-working on degrees in science, math, technology, and engineering. "Their research is totally beyond me, but we work with them on everything other than their research. We assist quite a few to retain them and help them with their unique situations-sometimes these students are so busy doing research and writing papers they lose track of some of the other things, like how to do a CV, or how to perform in a job interview," says Julio.

Through workshops and networking sessions, Julio combines social activities with professional development opportunities. Julio explains, "At the grad school level there isn't much of a social life, so we are trying to build that. It's different than what's happening at the undergrad level, where there are a lot of programs, dorm life, and activities constantly. But for grad students, a lot of them are older, they have families, they feel isolated because of their specialized research interests." These workshops provide an opportunity to develop the professional network that may be just as important to a successful career as a strong research background.

In addition to his work at UCSC, Julio maintains a strong connection to CSUMB. He teaches First Year Seminar, supporting first year students by ensuring they know about all of the different support programs and offices available to them at CSUMB. "I really wanted to make the students aware of what is available to them now, rather than hear them say 'well, I didn't know…' later on."

He has also organized opportunities for CSUMB students to meet with UCSC graduate students and faculty, giving them the chance to learn about various grad programs and get tips on how to apply. And, as if all that weren't enough, Julio also serves on the CSUMB Alumni Association Board of Directors as the newly elected Vice President. "My grounding was always working with these professors and staying close to people who could provide me advising and mentoring. Now, I want to be active and give back."

By following in his mother's footsteps, Julio made the Cardona-Raya family a CSUMB legacy. Now, through his work at CSUMB and UCSC, he continues to spread that educational legacy with a wide range of students-from first year and first generation all the way through to the Ph.D.

~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer/Web Editor
