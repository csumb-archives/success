--- layout: archive title: Denise Gant date: 2012-01-01 ---
From energy litigation to government surveillance, Denise Gant explores the complex world of computer security

For Denise Gant, the road to CSUMB has been long and winding.

Born and raised in Maryland, Denise graduated from high school and went straight into Morgan State University, a historically black college in Baltimore. After two years, she dropped out. "I needed to work."

And work she did, primarily in administrative positions. She married, had children, moved across the country, and eventually became a grandmother. When she was laid off from her job in publishing, she decided "it was a good time to pursue a different goal."

"In the '90s technology was booming and I decided to pursue that. I went to Heald College and was in the first class to receive the computer tech AA degree. Right before I finished at Heald, I got a job at Moss Landing Power Plant."

It was around this time that the California energy crisis was peaking. "I was hired through a temp agency to copy key people's data for the lawyers during all the power company litigation." Denise was originally hired for a three month period, but then the attacks of September 11, 2001 occurred. "My co-worker was in the Reserves. He was called out to duty and I held his place until he came back."

During this time, Denise worked as computer technician. "Help desk, teaching people how to use applications, getting new hires set up. I maintained inventory and fixed it when stuff when wrong. It was fun for me, an independent way of working, and I liked that."

When her coworker returned, Denise found that her position with Moss Landing was up. "I decided to come back to school and pursue a higher degree."

A TMAC major focused on networking, Denise finds security issues to be most interesting. "I took a class in wireless security and it was just so exciting. And then I took an advanced networking class where we continued wireless security and just network security."

With security breaches grabbing headlines, Denise and her class determined they should share their knowledge. "Considering the crisis with identity theft, and all that sort of thing, we can do something viable and give that to the community. We can write a paper for your grandmother that says, 'You want to use your computer, this is how you protect yourself.'"

Professor Robert van Spyk led the entire class in writing a white paper entitled Personal Protection: Crisis. Each student was assigned a threat or a solution to electronic security. Denise's threat: government surveillance.

"These are things you heard from people that they called paranoid. And then I started digging into it and I was just amazed. There has been surveillance of every single electronic signal that goes out-I'm talking your cell phone, your fax, your computer, whatever has an electronic signal.

"All across the globe people are upset. Very, very smart people are very upset about this whole scenario. I don't care if you hear anything I write or say on the phone, but it's the principle of the matter."

And the solution to government surveillance? Encryption. "I'm finding that people, especially people from Scandinavian countries who are very tech savvy and have very high degrees in computer science, have created resources to help. I know Canada was doing a lot to develop encryption software so that they could communicate confidentially. It's a beautiful, great thing-technology that addresses these issues."

To further increase her understanding of computers, Denise is also minoring in math. "Math is the language of computers. They don't speak alpha, they speak numeric, and I knew that it would help me in my field to know the logic."

Denise hopes to continue her studies in computers at CSUMB. "I want to go to grad school, and I'm thinking here because it's a continuum. I am happy with the level of interaction. I've been to other schools and a lot of those teachers didn't want that level of interaction. So I'm happy to be here."
