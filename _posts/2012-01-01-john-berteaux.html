--- layout: archive title: John Berteaux date: 2012-01-01 ---

Every month, philosophy professor John Berteaux writes a column for the Monterey County Herald detailing his latest musings on life. His topics often focus on race and other issues of diversity-a subject he teaches at CSUMB-but occasionally he writes about his travel experiences or evaluates the relevance of social traditions like the Homecoming Dance. You get the sense that John rarely lets a moment pass without pausing to question its meaning.

This sort of questioning has lead John down some interesting paths. Following a successful high school basketball career, John joined the Marines. After serving for four years, he was accepted to San Diego State as part of the first Affirmative Action class. He quickly fell in love with the classroom and became a serious student of philosophy and classical languages. After earning his bachelor's degree, John was accepted straight into a Ph.D. program in philosophy at UC San Diego. After a year in the program, however, John began to question the relevance of his field of study.

"Philosophy seemed to me to be very abstract and disconnected from the real world. I couldn't make a connection between the types of issues we were talking about in the classroom and what was happening around me in the seventies with the civil rights movement. So I quit and went into business," he says.

Over the next twenty years, John worked for a number of companies in marketing and sales roles, and eventually started his own marketing business. By the nineties, this path hit a dead end. His business was failing and John was facing bankruptcy, so he made the decision to chart a new course at age 47-he returned to graduate school at UC San Diego to earn his doctorate in philosophy.

"With the business under, with no money, trying to get a kid through college, I came home one day and said, 'I'm going to go back to graduate school and get a Ph.D.-not a Ph.D. in making money, but a Ph.D. in philosophy.'" John considers this to be one of his greatest accomplishments, along with convincing his spouse, Suzie, to "overcome all my inadequacies and stay around me for 36 years."

After earning his degree, John taught as a lecturer at both San Diego State and UC San Diego for several years, but after getting a full time tenure-track professorship at San Diego State, he realized it wasn't the right place for him. When he learned of the position at CSUMB, he was drawn to the university's Vision Statement and the opportunity to teach about diversity and social justice. John recalls, "When I read the job announcement and showed my dissertation advisor the job announcement, we both looked at it and said, 'this is the perfect job for you.'"

The new opportunities John found at CSUMB meant new challenges as well. The emphasis on integrating technology into the classroom forced him to explore different ways of teaching. John was initially somewhat resistant, but by working closely with Kalpesh Patel and the Center for Academic Technologies, John began utilizing tablet PCs in his courses. "When it was first mentioned to me, I thought, 'Eh, I don't think so.' But I gave it a try, got frustrated, and turned it off. But it was something I kept coming back to again and again. After two years using tablets, they are now a mainstay in my classroom. The first thing I do is go in and hookup my tablet."

CSUMB's commitment to service learning presented another opportunity for John to grow as a teacher. In an article he wrote for the Campus Chronicles, John details the challenges of cutting short his lectures to allow more time for students to participate in activities that promote critical self-reflection.

In addition to reflection on service learning activities, John works to make sure his students don't run into the same problem he did as a graduate student in the seventies. He illustrates how philosophy is connected to real world issues by having students discuss the ethical dilemmas embedded in current events. For example, he might bring in a newspaper article about Americans who don't have health insurance to launch a discussion of the distribution of health care in our society and around the world. By applying the theories of philosophers like Alastair Macintyre or Thomas Hobbes to current events, John's students test the strengths and weaknesses of various philosophical models.

John has further reconciled the disconnect between philosophy and reality in his role as a member of the Bioethics Board at Natividad Medical Center in Salinas. The board examines the ethical questions that arise when doctors have to make difficult decisions about how to treat patients.

"You come to the hospital, you're a Jehovah's Witness, let's say, and you need a blood transfusion. What are the ethical issues surrounding that? What can we do, what can't we do? It doesn't have to be Jehovah's Witnesses, it could be that you're coming into the hospital as a pregnant woman and making decisions about your life or the life of the child. That's really where those issues in the hospital arise." The Bioethics Board reviews how doctors make these challenging choices and their discussion in turn shapes hospital policy and future decision making processes.

John's incessant questioning has led him down many interesting paths, and continues to do so. His curiosity has taken him to Cuba, Panama, and Venezuela; the disputed territories of the Middle East including the West Bank; and various barbershops in the Monterey Bay area in search of answers. Here at CSUMB, we are thankful that his meandering path has converged with ours, and are confident that many more interesting questions lie ahead.

~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer, Web Editor
