"…Dr. Mendoza gave a short overview of the history of the Royal Chapel…then we began to break down the concrete at the foundation of the building…We used a hammer, chisel, and sledgehammer and were careful not to break into the adobe that was beneath the surface…Underneath the dirt my colleague discovered two pieces of pottery (possibly from the same artifact) and two pieces of glass and mudstone…this was my first time ever on an actual excavation site…"

-Excerpted from a student online journal entry

Dr. Ruben Mendoza delights in student journal entries like this. When he came to CSUMB as a founding faculty member in 1995, he helped develop CSUMB's highly hands-on, project- and outcomes-based curriculum-a curriculum that allows students to have experiences at working archaeological digs from the start. More than a decade later, Ruben takes great pride in remaining committed to CSUMB's unique curriculum.

Today Ruben oversees digs at the Royal Presidio in Monterey and the missions in San Juan Bautista and Carmel, where students quickly find themselves working in the dirt and discovering historic objects. On one occasion, they came across a 19th century copper cigarette holder, and on another a Spanish silver coin dated to 1779, but more often students find the foundations of ancient buildings and items such as pottery shards…and every now and then someone might locate counterfeit gold coins. "I do play pranks and plant things once in a while," laughs Ruben.

On a more serious note, the work being done at these sites does have an important impact on our understanding of area history. "We've had major findings that have turned existing research on its head," says Ruben. One example was the discovery of poorly-fired roof tiles at the Presidio site. These salmon-colored tiles contradicted the existing belief that the roof of the building was a deep red color. The recovery of the aforementioned Spanish "half real" silver coin from foundation fill in turn served to date the original perimeter defensive wall at the Presidio to 1779.

In addition to chisels and sledgehammers, archaeology students use some of the most high-tech equipment available. Many classes use wireless tablet PCs that allow them to upload their field notes, journals, and specimen data on-site. You can read these field journals on the Archaeology Institute website.

Another group of fifteen students took these wireless tools, database programs, barcode scanners, and related imaging technologies to the museum at Mission San Juan Bautista, where they cataloged the museum's collection and redeveloped an archive of historical books and.documents dating from 1500-1700. Thanks to their efforts, these materials are now available for scholarly research.

Ruben has found that overseeing all these projects and working extensively with students in the field has energized his writing; he has seven forthcoming publications this academic year alone. It makes for a good balance-Ruben demanding the best from his students, and in turn, the students bringing out the best in him.

~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer/Web Editor
