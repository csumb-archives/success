--- layout: archive title: Steven Levinson date: 2012-01-01 ---

In the eleven years that Steven Levinson has been teaching at CSUMB, he's been involved in almost every part of campus in some way. He's taught courses in Teledramatic Arts and Technology, Service Learning, Visual and Public Arts, and Liberal Learning, and been a guest lecturer in Human Communication and Social and Behavioral Sciences classes. He's also served as the interim project manager of the Soledad Street Revitalization project, and workd as the director of aquisitions for the Fort Ord Museum and Archive. His television and radio projects have also put him in contact with people from almost every CSUMB department. And if that isn't enough, he also serves as the faculty advisor to several student clubs.

"I'm a bit harder to define than other people on campus," says Levinson. "But I'm very serious about cross-campus collaboration. I cross a lot of different walls."

The potential to cross traditional campus boundaries sparked Levinson's initial interest in teaching at CSUMB. He explains, "I was teaching video at San Francisco State when I went to a conference and CSUMB had a table there. I looked through the catalog and noticed this new department with this name I didn't understand, but that had radio and television and theatre. I was excited that all were in one umbrella, so I'd be able to take advantage of a lot of different kinds of work I had done."

While in earning his broadcast electronic communication arts (BECA) degree, Levinson also began experimenting with the combination of multimedia elements on stage. "I'd been involved in theatre-in set design, tech support, and acting-with Teatro Latino and Teatro Esperanza, two then-San Francisco-based Latino community groups," says Levinson. "I started to experiment with more than your standard theatre production, with the incorporation of video and cameras, the interactivity with the stage and the screen, which is something we've done a lot with here [in TAT] as well."

In addition to theatre and media-making, Steven has also utilized his background as a community-builder in his contributions to CSUMB. "My family was very involved politically. The whole concept of community support and working with community organizations, which is essential to this university, was natural to me, because that's how I grew up." As an aspiring ten-year-old artist and community activist in Berkeley, Steven even designed a poster for one of the Black Panthers' campaigns. "It was Huey's favorite poster, actually," says Steven.

"It wasn't a stretch for me to teach service learning, to try to express ideas of social justice and the importance of community work because it was just what I'd always done. I didn't have to learn service learning; it was a part of who I was when I got here."

In Fall 2006, Steven brought those community building skills to his role as interim project manager of the Soledad Street Revitalization project in Salinas. This semester he returns to Soledad Street in a new role: "This particular semester I am teaching a class for the Service Learning Institute itself, doing oral histories of Soledad Street, Chinatown, and Japantown, making contact with the primarily Chinese and Japanese people who historically have lived in that neighborhood, and actually recording oral histories of the Asians who have lived there the last 30-50 years."

Whatever the subject, Steven approaches teaching from a problem-solving perspective. "I try to get students to learn enough so that they can handle the problem rather than just answering the question," says Steven. It's a technique that he learned one of his professors and mentors, Nancy "Sammy" Reist.

"I remember at one point I was having a problem with an audio board-I wasn't hearing some signal and I asked, 'What's the problem?' She said, 'Look at the board.' She wouldn't answer the question; she just said, 'Look at the board.' I had almost exactly the same situation with a student who was having almost exactly the same problem-a certain button was pushed that was limiting what you could hear elsewhere on the board. It was almost the exact same conversation I had with that student that Sammy had with me fifteen years ago. The first few years when I was teaching, whenever there was an issue I would stop and think, 'What would Sammy do in this situation?'"

Because of this approach, Steven has become a mentor to his students in the same way Sammy served as a mentor for him. He remains in contact with many of his former students and has built a strong TAT alumni network by organizing formal and informal alumni events. "I was down [in L.A. recently] and we had a little bowling party, 20 to 25 people showed up, but it was a real spread-people who graduated in '98, people who hadn't graduated yet, people looking for jobs, people with jobs, people in a position to hire. It was just a chance to sit with each other, find out what we're doing, have fun, and potentially network about real job possibilities."

Steven's wide-ranging interests do perhaps make him difficult to define, but his overarching commitment to collaboration, connection, community, and learning serve him well in his many endeavors. By crossing traditional boundaries, Steven has synthesized a successful career and made a difference that can be seen in the success of his students and on just about every part of campus.

 

~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer, Web Editor
