Greg Ruiz ('05)
A use for fog

Ever heard an annoying drip . . . drip . . . drip from your rain gutter on foggy mornings? What if some of that water could be put to use rather than wasted?

Greg Ruiz (far left, with Dan Fernandez), a December 2005 Earth Systems Science & Policy B.S. graduate, devised an experiment to answer that question. He
set up three "fog collectors"-one on campus, two in Big Sur -- to determine the feasibility of reclaiming useful amounts of water from fog.

From July to December, he logged the amount of water collected, then analyzed the data to see if the technology might be useful in Monterey County. Although large amounts of water were not collected, he concluded that such a system would prove beneficial in irrigating a backyard garden.

The principle is simple: moisture-laden fog collects on mesh (in Ruiz's experiment, about three square feet) and condenses into water, which trickles through a tube into a container. Commercial fog collectors -- such as those used to augment domestic water supplies in Chile, Oman and South Africa -- are much larger.

Dr. Dan Fernandez, associate professor in Science and Environmental Policy, was the inspiration for the project. He had read about the technology several years ago and wondered if the simple concept could be adapted to help with the area's water needs. The results of Ruiz's work provided that answer.

Ruiz's project was funded by a National Science Foundation Alliance for Minority Participation (AMP) scholarship. The goal of the scholarship program is to increase the number of underrepresented minority students in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics and to help those students prepare for graduate school.

Six CSUMB students have earned AMP scholarships in the last two years. Last summer, students used the scholarships to study at the Scripps' Summer Training Academy for Research in the Sciences, the Hatfield Marine Center at Oregon State University, and Stanford's Hopkins Marine Station.
