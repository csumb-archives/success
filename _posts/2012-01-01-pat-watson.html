Tech-savvy artist helps students find new modes of expression

In the late '80s, Pat Watson became convinced that the Macintosh computer was the right tool for a project that merged photographs and text. He paid $2,300 for the best computer available-a MacPlus with a screen the size of a postcard. On that day he stepped into the world of digital art -and hasn't looked back.

Watson came to CSUMB in 1999 after teaching at Sierra Nevada College near Lake Tahoe. It was a fortuitous moment: Watson wanted to move to the Monterey Bay area and CSUMB had an opening for someone with his unique expertise in digital art and design.

Watson keeps close track of the new boundaries being forged in this emerging genre. "There is some very adventurous stuff going on…[This generation] has grown up in a screen-based world-weaned on the screen by commercials, TV titles, and music videos. You can see that as a negative if you want, but I see it as an opportunity for people to be creative in a different realm. A lot of very intriguing work is being done with, for example, image sampling in the same way hip hop is sound sampling. A lot of the work is very personal, expressive, challenging, and abstract."

Among the students, Watson is famous for his digital art and design course that entails studying the abstract visual qualities of letter forms and a project titled "Postmodern Me" in which students "apply philosophy and theory to a representation of how they feel and think about who they are at this point in time in the cosmos." Pretty heavy stuff.

In addition to digital art and design, Watson teaches a course in 3D animation which resulted in what he considers to be his greatest accomplishment at CSUMB-the Tanimura & Antle Family Memorial Library flythrough. Watson led a team of three students in the creation of a three-dimensional visualization of the future library. It was an immensely challenging project for both Watson and the students. "It did turn out to be a colossal undertaking. It really helps people understand the kind of work that they do at DreamWorks and Pixar and ILM."

With the flythrough completed, Watson is spending the summer writing a book on digital art. Always one to embrace technology, Watson utilizes an iPod to finetune his writing. He records himself reading his latest chapter, then plays it back, listening for areas that need improvement. "A lot of the ideas in the book have been percolating around in my mind for quite a while. Now I'm just trying to massage those into a more coherent form." When the book is complete, he plans to podcast each chapter and then use the podcasts in his courses.

There is more to Watson, though, than technology junkie. An avid surfer since 1965, Watson still manages to hit the waves on an almost daily basis. Growing up in Venice and Long Beach instilled a deep love of the ocean in him. "I love the beach as much now as I ever did. It's still such a treat to go out and watch the waves and be in the sand and just be a part of the whole experience."

Before the MacPlus, Watson was involved in more traditional art forms: photography and painting. His work has been displayed in galleries and the Nevada Museum of Fine Art. He also previously taught photography, drawing, and painting. When not teaching, he worked as a designer, both freelance and with an ad agency.

Ultimately, for Watson, his teaching and his career come down to a celebration of creativity and expression. "I've tried to really incorporate as much about art and creativity and design as I could."

~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer/Web Editor
