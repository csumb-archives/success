Scholarships, passion, and fast food lead to growing math program

With professors like Hongde Hu, it's no wonder CSUMB's math program is growing. His passion for math seems contagious. "He's like the Energizer™ bunny. He's exciting," says Monica Galloway, a student taking her first class with Hongde this summer.

Or maybe it's the food.

"Hongde is always taking us to get food. He loves taking his classes to different Chinese buffets," relates math major Pam Johnson. Once, when she and two fellow students were deeply engrossed in solving a problem for the Mathematical Contest in Modeling, Hongde told them he was going to get them some really good Mexican food.

"He came back with Taco Bell," Pam says with a laugh.

The combination of Hongde's infectious enthusiasm and MSG may be the initial draw, but it is his commitment to students, particularly minority students, that keeps them involved. "You have to show them you are committed to them," says Hongde. "You have to connect with the student-that's most important. Many of my students are busy with work; they don't have time to contact me. So I have to contact the student, make sure they are set."

One way Hongde makes sure his students are set is by helping them secure scholarships that allow them to focus on their studies and prepare for careers or graduate school. "Some of the students come from very poor families. My students sometimes work too many hours so it's hard for them to come in and study well. That's why we try to get them scholarships – so they don't need to work 50-60 hours a week," Hongde explains.

A major source of scholarships for Hongde's students is the Louis Stokes Alliance for Minority Participation program, a project led on CSUMB's campus by Dr. William Head, a professor in the Division of Science and Environmental Policy. Funded by the National Science Foundation, LSAMP's goal is to increase the number of students from underrepresented minority groups earning degrees in science and math, and encourage students to pursue graduate degrees in those fields.

This summer, with LSAMP funding, Hongde is working closely with about a dozen students to help them produce top level undergraduate work-and position them to get into the best graduate programs.

Pam Johnson, a Native American scholarship recipient, will spend the summer working on research for her Senior Capstone, the final project which represents the culmination of her education at CSUMB. She will also prepare to again participate in the Mathematical Contest in Modeling. Last year, she and her Taco Bell-fueled team took home the second-highest honor in a field of 970 colleges from around the world, all competing by using mathematical models to solve real world problems.

Brenda Thomas and Monica Galloway, both African-American reentry students, will attend summer workshops to prepare for the GRE. They also hope to attend some conferences and get a preview of what different graduate schools offer. "I don't think I'll stop until I get my doctorate. I'm going to be like Hongde. I want to eventually teach math at a university or junior college," says Brenda.

Thanks to LSAMP-supported summer workshops and a research internship with Hongde, David Stansell may graduate a year early-if he doesn't decide to spend next year studying abroad in Japan.

Other students will attend workshops that prepare them to serve as math tutors in three predominantly Hispanic local high schools. This has the double benefit of reinforcing the skills of the tutors and exposing high school students to mentors who have successfully continued their studies in college.

All have almost daily contact with Hongde.

For Hongde, teaching wasn't always this way. Prior to joining CSUMB in 1999, he taught at the University of Pennsylvania. "It's a totally different kind of environment. There, I couldn't remember any students' names-with 200 students I just showed up. I had 10 TAs. I was only there for three hours, just taught one class, that's all. Professors are busy with their own business, research, traveling," Hongde explains.

Hongde arrived on the West Coast with a job offer at Stanford. But then he learned about a job at CSUMB: "They didn't have a math minor or major. They only offered calculus and discrete math. I thought it was really, really interesting that the university was new. When I interviewed I found the colleagues here just amazing, so I decided to come."

Together with department chair Don Pierce, Hongde expanded the math program. In 2002, the math minor was approved. New faculty members were hired. More and more students chose the math minor. Then, in 2004, the major was approved. In two years, the major grew to 50 students, a number Hongde hopes will double.

"I established a math club in 2004-we had a bowling party and another party in the BBC so students can get some rest, do some math, do some talking, do some work," says Hongde. "The whole point is to get more students interested in math."

Hongde's winning recipe of personal attention, scholarships, bowling, expertise and enthusiasm for the subject, and fast food has hooked a growing number of students. If he continues dishing it out, he'll have success in the bag.

~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer/Web Editor
