Success Stories ~ The Three Mathketeers
Students honored in international math competition

Think of it as "NUMB3RS" with an agricultural twist.

While the hit TV show uses complicated math to solve crimes, a team of students at CSUMB used it to solve a problem involving irrigation of fields, and won a top academic honor in the process.

The team-Christina Schmunk ('06 ESSP and MATH), Pamela Johnson ('07 MATH), and Jonathan Offi ('07 MATH)-had 89 hours to tackle one of three questions in the Mathematical Contest in Modeling, a complex problem-solving competition. The students chose a problem with real-world applications for the local area-how to design an irrigation system that would minimize labor and maximize efficiency and uniformity of water distribution.

The problems are designed to give students a glimpse of what applied mathematicians might do outside of academia. The problems are taken from the fields of science, engineering, and industry.

The team won a Meritorious Award, the second-highest honor, which places them in the top 15 percent of the nearly 1,000 teams that entered the competition. Those teams represented more than 500 colleges worldwide, including Harvard, Oxford, Duke, the U.S. Military Academy, and a number of universities in China.

The teams downloaded the problem from the Internet at 8 p.m. (Eastern time) on Feb. 2, 2006, and then had four days to work out a solution.

They didn't get much sleep during the long weekend of work, and ate a lot of fast food. "I'd bring them pizzas," said team adviser and math professor Dr. Hongde Hu, "but I advised them not to stay up all night-they needed a clear mind to tackle the problem."

The students did some experiments and field activities during the four days, using a wireless tablet computer to design the model, then explained their thought process in a 14-page paper-including diagrams and graphs-that was submitted to the contest judges. With so many entries, it takes several months to score the papers; this year's results were just released.

Said team member Jonathan Offi: "The really nice thing about the team was how well we worked together. We were never even close to arguing with each other."

The university has done well in the contest in the past. CSUMB has entered annually since 2003; it earned honorable mentions in 2003 and 2005. "When we see student success, we want more. And it's good for the faculty-students push us, like we push them. Now, we want to go to the next level-the top 1 percent-and earn an 'outstanding' award," said Dr. Hu.

That sentiment was shared by Offi.

"I have one more year at CSUMB, so that means I have one more competition to achieve the outstanding award that I haven't yet received," said Offi, who was a member of the 2003 team. "I think this is an extremely prestigious competition and I am happy to have been a part of it more than once."

At least one member of the local ag community has expressed an interest in the university's mathematical research: Golden Field Greenhouses of Castroville consulted Dr. Hu for help in figuring out its most efficient product mix. Dr. Hu says his students are always looking for real-world problems, and would be open to requests for help from other members of the local agricultural community.

The contest is funded by a number of prestigious organizations including the National Security Agency, the National Science Foundation, and the Mathematical Association of America.

Here's the summary of the students' solution:

A farmer is interested in minimizing the amount of time he needs to spend maintaining his irrigation system. Our goal is to design an irrigation system and schedule that will minimize labor and maximize efficiency and uniformity of watering radius.

With a "hand move" lateral sprinkler system (20m pipe with 10cm diameter), we will be watering an eighty-meter by thirty-meter lot that we separated into 3 plots (p1, p2, p3). The sprinkler pipe has three Rainbird 18 Series VAN type sprinklers that are evenly spaced at ten-meters apart. The spray radius of each sprinkler is 5m (10m diameter) creating minimal overlapping vertically.

Our model consists of fifteen laterals movements across the field, 5 rotations per plot. We constructed a schedule of rotation for the plots using p1 (30m x 30m), p2 (30m x 30m), and p3 (20m x 30m). To maximize efficiency and uniformity of p1, we created 5 lateral movements. Evenly spaced, we positioned the pipe centered with 5m gaps on each side. Position 1 starts 5m from the top of the plot and rotates down to the 15m mark and then to the 25m mark. This evenly primes the field without flooding any one spot. Position 4 comes back up to the 8m mark and then jumps down to 22m mark.

The union of the spray radius of rows 4 and 5 creates an overlap of about 65% with rows 1, 2, and 3 which we found optimal in our research. The overlap of the spray radius will cover the few gaps (no direct water) in p1, without flooding any one point. We will use the same rotation for p2 and p3. P3 has a different dimension (20mx30m), so the third sprinkler in plot 3 will be turned off to avoid un-necessary watering.

By keeping within the parameters given, our model says that no part of the field will receive more than 0.67cm of water per hour and every part of the field will receive 2.01cm per 4 days. The labor involved is 2 minutes to rotate the pipe every 3hours and 45 minutes. Our assumptions include the soil type, environmental conditions, and the lack of pressure loss on our system.
