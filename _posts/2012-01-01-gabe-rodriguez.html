Success Stories ~ Gabe Rodriguez '08

The Making of a Scientist

Perhaps Gabe Rodriguez was born to be a scientist. At an early age, he exhibited scientific tendencies. "My mother tells me that before I could form a sentence, I would sit in the back yard, pick rocks up, drop them, observe their motion and declare, 'Gravity,' " Gabe says.

Today, he looks forward to continuing his research as he pursues a doctorate at UC Santa Barbara. The recipient of a very prestigious and highly competitive fellowship from the National Science Foundation, Gabe will join the Department of Ecology, Evolution, and Marine Biology.

For him, however, the road from Baby Newton to graduate student wasn't quite a straight line. Over the years he imagined himself as an astronaut, mechanic, engineer, teacher, and just plain confused.

By 1999, he was struggling to get through community college and working 20 to 40 hours per week in Los Angeles machine shops to cover the cost of his education. "I had little focus and no clear goals," Gabe says. His grades reflected his busy schedule and distracted mind.

At that point, Gabe did some soul searching and rediscovered his passion for science, specifically environmental science. "I feel that the most important things in all our lives, aside from our families, are the planet we walk on, the air we breathe, and the oceans that surround us," he says.

Armed with a fresh sense of purpose, Gabe structured his life to revolve around science. He volunteered at the Cabrillo Marine Aquarium as a tour guide and began searching out a university program in marine science. The interdisciplinary Earth Systems Science & Policy program at CSUMB matched his interests perfectly. He transferred in 2005 and graduates this May.

While a student, Gabe took every opportunity he could to get hands-on experience in the field -- which proved pivotal to his career. He spent two academic quarters engaged in marine science research at Shannon Point Marine Center in Anacortes, Wash.

"The entire experience was very challenging and I loved every minute," Gabe says, "usually being the first to arrive at the lab and often the last to leave. Until then, my decision to study the marine environment was largely based on a non-academic affinity for the coast in southern California. At Shannon Point, I realized what research really was and it became a fervent passion."

Following that experience, he applied for and received a NOAA Hollings scholarship that allowed him to participate in a research internship studying invasive alga in the Monterey Bay National Marine Sanctuary with NOAA scientist Dr. Steve Lonhart. He presented the results of this project at the Hollings Scholar Conference, and is also engaged to present it to the Western Society of Naturalists in November.

Those key research experiences helped Gabe land the National Science Foundation fellowship, which will provide $30,000 per year for three years to support his continuing education.

"For me, a bachelor's degree is only the beginning," he says. "I realize that graduate school will be a lot of work, but I am confident that I am adequately prepared for the experience and eagerly anticipate the challenge."


~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer/Web Editor
