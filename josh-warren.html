Josh Warren '05

It's hard to box Josh Warren in. He's a computer technician, journalist, community advocate and semi-pro basketball player. He's also weighing the option of Harvard for graduate school.

Josh came to CSUMB from the San Francisco in 2003 for two simple reasons: "I was recruited to play basketball and the school seemed like a good fit, being heavy in technology. I was always interested in technology."

Today, those interests still define Josh's life. He works as a senior technician for Auto Warehousing, a company in Tacoma, Wash., that manages the import and export of vehicles. "Vehicles are shipped to the U.S, from overseas in containers. We pull them off the boats, add accessories, allocate them and get them to the dealerships," Josh says.

That process requires a lot of technology, which Josh works to keep running from the corporate office. "I help oversee the computers and phones, and support users in 26 facilities-including three in Canada and one in Mexico," Josh says. "It's really pretty critical to the company's success."

He credits his education at CSUMB for giving him the ability to adapt to the demands of his job.

"The Information Technology and Communication Design major gives you a good foundation in all computers," he says. "The cool thing about CSUMB is they teach you how to teach yourself and learn. In school I was more into multimedia and design, but now my job is more technical, more administration and hardware. The major gives you the background, teaches you the logic."

Josh, who graduated in 2005, also minored in journalism, and after arriving in Seattle he put his journalism skills to work for the urban magazine SeaSpot. "Music was my main focus," he says. "I wrote album reviews and got to interview famous people like Russell Simmons." Though he no longer writes regularly for the magazine, he does occasionally do some freelance work for them.

Outside of work, he still spends a lot of time on the basketball court, playing point guard for the Jazz of Tacoma, a semi-pro team competing in the International Basketball League.

Before joining the Jazz, Josh volunteered for the Seattle chapter of the NAACP, where he helped organize a group of high school students to compete in the Afro-Academic, Cultural, Technological and Scientific Olympics (ACT-SO). The ACT-SO program encourages high academic and cultural achievements among high school students and gives these students a venue for recognition.

"You have to keep advancing yourself," he says. "Be a part of the community and help out."

Up next Josh is considering graduate school, specifically a one-year program through the Harvard School of Education. CSUMB professor Valerie Landau recommended him to the program, he says, "But I'm not a 100 percent sure."

Though he isn't sure what that next step will be, Josh is certain he's followed the right path.
"I've got a good job that I like with a growing company," he says. "I've got sports. I can't complain."


~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer/Web Editor
