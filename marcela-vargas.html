Success Stories ~ Marcela Vargas

As the child of an immigrant family from Mexico, Marcela Vargas has faced a variety of challenges.

She came to the United States when she was 16, not knowing any English, but eager for the promise of joining American society.

Her biggest obstacle was learning the language. Since no one in her family spoke English, she had no one with whom to practice. "Those first months were the most demanding, knotty, and challenging," she said. "Sometimes, I felt very desperate and lost, but I never gave up.

"My parents work in the fields, so they didn't know how to help me. Besides that, after school I went straight home to cook for my family and do the house chores before they came back from work.

"And I worked a part-time job that took most of the time I needed to do homework."

Despite the hardships and challenges, she completed Alisal High School in seven semesters.

Some months after starting at Alisal, " . . . I was named Student of the Month," and that "became my motivation to continue working even harder," she said.

Graduating from Alisal with a 3.5 grade-point average, she entered CSUMB and did even better - compiling a 3.89 GPA in her first semester.

Marcela is CSUMB's nominee for the 2007 William Randolph Hearst/California State University Trustees' Award for outstanding achievement. She was selected from a competitive pool of students for her exceptional academic performance and commitment to education and community service.

Marcela plans to major in Collaborative Health and Human Services because "I want to help low- income families learn about the resources and benefits available to them."

She enjoys performing community service. "I volunteered to construct a playground in Salinas for the children of migrant workers, in order for them to have a recreational place to use as a family. And I volunteered to give information about the High School Equivalency Program to people who are interested in obtaining their GED."

Marcela's personal goals are mainly about her parents. "I want to get an education to help them" financially, she said. "They are getting older, and they are already suffering the consequences from their hard work in the fields, such as back problems. I want to give back a little of what I accomplished thanks to them.

And, "I'm the first in my family to go to college; I would like to be a role model for my brother, who's a senior in high school."

She's adamant about her educational path. "I plan to graduate from CSUMB in four years and pursue a master's degree. For my career, I want to become a nurse or a nutritionist because I have a great interest in health and in helping people overcome their challenges in life."

CSU Trustees will hold an awards presentation Sept. 18 for the Hearst scholars - students who have demonstrated financial need, experienced personal hardships and have attributes of merit, including superior academic performance, exemplary community service and significant personal achievements.

One of them will receive a $6,000 scholarship; some of them will receive $3,000 awards.

CSUMB students have had success in the Hearst competition in recent years. The last two nominees - Daniel Delacruz in 2005 and Adriana Pacheco in 2006 - were each awarded $3,000.

~ Joan Weiner, News and Public Information Officer
