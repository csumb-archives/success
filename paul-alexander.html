Paul Alexander '05

SBS Professor Paul Alexander can relate to the problems students come to him with during office hours-just two years ago, he was a student himself.

Paul graduated from CSUMB in 2005 with a degree in Social and Behavioral Sciences. He went straight into an accelerated master's degree program in anthropology at the University of Chicago, earning his master's degree in a single year. After that, Paul returned to CSUMB, this time as a faculty member.

"My favorite part of the job is working one on one with students and being able to mentor them," says Paul. "I can relate to what the students are going through because I went through it not that long ago myself. I know that it can be frustrating and stressful, but it should be stressful-graduating from college should not be a walk through the park. But it's helpful that I can relate to that and know what it's like. Just a year ago I was stressing through my master's thesis and finishing classes for grad school, so I am very close to that feeling."

Paul knows how important faculty mentors are; if it weren't for the mentors he found as a student in the SBS department, he may never have discovered his passion for intellectual study.

Paul wasn't the greatest student in high school, and after a two-year stint in a junior college he figured he was finished with his education. "I was out of school for three years, working full time at for a corporate giant when I hit the ceiling at my job. At that point I knew that if I wanted to do something that was appealing to me, I'd need a degree," says Paul.

Paul chose CSUMB because it was close enough to his family in Morgan Hill, but far enough away to feel like a change. He also liked the small class sizes, on-campus housing, and overall affordability. But the most important factor Paul discovered was the freedom to direct his own academic experience.

"I didn't excel in high school because I wasn't given the freedom to do what I wanted; I felt limited by the structure. I didn't work in well in environments where I was told what to do. I did my job and could function, but I had no passion for it," he says.

"When I started getting close to my faculty members and mentors I could see that there was a freedom in working with your mind and being an intellectual, using your mind as your main tool of operation. One of my professors once said 'I own my means of production; there is no one who can tell me what to do with my mind.' The idea of focusing the whole of my attention onto the kind of problems I'm interested in-that concept was very appealing to me."

Professor Manuel Carlos helped him explore this idea further. Paul explains, "I was a teaching assistant for Professor Carlos and he really mentored me on what it meant to be a part of academia, what it meant to be an intellectual, and helped me clarify what it was I wanted to do."

And so his career trajectory was set. In graduate school he developed a research agenda centered on immigration and completed his thesis on the impact of the Mexican Consulate on the lives of Mexican citizens living in Chicago.

Now, in addition to teaching, Paul continues his research on the Mexican immigrant experience: "I study a lot in Watsonville, right up the coast. The anthropology faculty has been working in Watsonville for almost seven years, studying the community, looking at how people use public space, especially in the downtown area, and looking at the lives of Mexican immigrants who live in the city."

Paul is working on two archive projects to showcase the research he and other SBS faculty members and students have done. One includes audio, video, photographs, and writing about the Watsonville community. The second project includes a photography exhibit that celebrates CSUMB's relationship with Universidad Autonoma de Queretaro in Mexico. Student and faculty exchanges between the two universities began shortly after CSUMB was founded and this archive and exhibit provide a historical perspective on the research that's been done between the two institutions.

Between the research, archive projects, and teaching, Paul puts his mental means of production to good use. He recalls in impact of his professors, now his colleagues, and reflects, "Finding your vocation in life is not an easy thing by any stretch of the imagination. It's the mentors we find on our way who can point out what we are good at and point out what we might be able to do. They did that for me here as a student, and as a grad student, and now coming back here they've been there every step of the way."

~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer/Web Editor
