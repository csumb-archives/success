James Anderson & Ashley Keels

James Anderson '08 & Ashley Keels '08

 

"I don't think I would have been able to get through without him," Ashley Keels says about her husband, James "Jimmy" Anderson.

"The only reason I graduated with my GPA was, not so much that I had to compete with her, but I saw what she did and I was like, 'Oh wow, I better do as well,'" says Jimmy. "I can't have her showing me up in front of the dog."

In May 2008, Ashley and Jimmy graduated from CSUMB summa cum laude. Now, Ashley, Jimmy, the dog, the rabbit and the fish are headed to the University of Wisconsin at Madison, where Ashley and Jimmy will pursue graduate degrees -- Ashley a Ph.D. in cell and molecular biology, Jimmy a law degree. Both received Advanced Opportunity Fellowships, which cover full tuition. The dog will continue to keep an eye on Jimmy's GPA.

Early Obstacles

From an early age, both Ashley and Jimmy had obstacles to overcome.

Until the age of seven, Ashley lived in a rough neighborhood of Richmond, California. Surrounded by drugs and gang violence, she committed to a different path -- one of study and hard work. "As a black girl growing up in a dangerous environment, there were many difficult and trying times for my family and me," Ashley says. "They drove me to stay focused on my goals. Despite all the obstacles that have been placed in my way, and despite all the expectations society may have of me as an African American female, I overcame them and paved my way to college."

Jimmy grew up in Patterson, California, a small town in the Central Valley. As a biracial person who identifies most closely with his Mexican American/Chicano heritage, he had to confront many stereotypes and expectations about who he was and how he should act. His mother, a Mexican immigrant, learned English by watching Sesame Street with Jimmy between her shifts at a vegetable packing plant while his father, a white American, worked long hours as a truck driver. Both of Jimmy's parents worked hard, but his family always struggled financially. "Their struggle has inspired me never to quit in the face of adversity and I have taken this with me to college," Jimmy says.

Coldstone Connection

In 1994, Ashley's family moved to Modesto, about 40 minutes from Patterson. In high school, she worked at the Modesto Coldstone Creamery, and one day she was called down to sub for a sick employee at the Turlock location -- where Jimmy happened to work. After work that day, he asked her on a date. They've been together ever since.

College Bound

"When I met Jimmy I wasn't aiming to go college outside of the local junior college," Ashley says. "I basically came to CSUMB because he did."

Jimmy decided on CSUMB after visiting several universities. "It came down to economics," he says. "CSUMB was affordable and I loved the location."

From the start, Ashley knew she wanted to major in Earth Systems Science & Policy. "I found that I had a real passion for research in the sciences," she says. "This passion combined with the confidence I have gained from faculty support has encouraged my appetite for discovery."

As Ashley became more involved in the sciences, she took on a research project to develop a way to identify cyanobacteria that, when triggered, produce toxins that are harmful to wildlife and humans.

With guidance from three faculty members, Ashley worked on the molecular aspect of the project, creating a test to identify which genes were responsible for producing toxins. "The DNA and genetics stuff, that's what I think is really interesting," she says. "This research has given me the opportunity to learn about genetics and biology in a hands-on, real world setting as opposed to reading about it in a textbook, which has given me a much deeper understanding."

Meanwhile, Jimmy had set his sights on law school and designed his own curriculum through the Integrated Studies major. "Integrated Studies gave me the flexibility to do what I wanted to do," he says. "I combined Global Studies with Human Communication to get some international relations as well as history and prelaw courses."

Outside of class, Jimmy took a job at a local law firm to bolster his experience. "Working on various legal projects has shown me how difficult a lawyer's job is but also how rewarding it can be," he says.

The Next Step

Up next, graduate school. Because of their strong qualifications, the pair sweated through the application and selection process, facing the unique problem of too many choices.

"We applied to so many schools," says Jimmy. "Ashley applied to 21 schools; I applied to 22."

"We were praying to both get into at least one. We didn't want to go to separate schools," Ashley says. "And then we got eight matches…and then life got hard."

"We definitely had competing interests," Jimmy says.

"The best law schools do not necessarily have the best science programs and the best science programs do not necessarily have the best law schools," Ashley adds.

"We kind of met in the middle with Wisconsin," says Jimmy. "It's a really amazing institution for both of us and we could both do what we wanted. And we loved Madison."

Family Impact

Though the move to Madison will be a big change for the couple, they, and their families, are proud and looking forward to their future accomplishments.

"I'm the first scientist in my family by far. We have a lot of teachers, but nobody's pursued a higher degree, especially a Ph.D., especially in something like science," says Ashley.

She has been something of an inspiration. "Since I've been at CSUMB, my mom has gotten her associate degree," she says. "When I talk to them about what I do, they don't always get it, but they think it's really cool and are really excited."

Likewise, Jimmy's mother has started on her bachelor's degree in Spanish at the local junior college. She has plans to transfer to the local CSU.

"Both my parents are excited about where I am going and love to tell all their friends and boost me up," he says. "They are really happy and really proud."

Long-term goals

If all goes as planned, Jimmy will finish his program in three years. Ashley's, however, will take six. Their ultimate goal is to return to California, where Ashley hopes to complete her post doctorate research at one of several prestigious institutions. "We have Scripps; we have Berkeley; we have UC San Francisco - really great schools to do research," she says.

Parting advice

Looking back, Jimmy offers this advice to students: "It's what you make of a situation. Both Ashley and I worked really, really, really hard and we ended up at one of the top universities in the nation. Top universities do recruit from schools like CSUMB; they do want students from these backgrounds. Try to find what's right for you and work really hard and you can do it."


~ Liz MacDonald, Senior Writer/Web Editor
